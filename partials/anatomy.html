The oral veil is lined with small bumps called "papillae". These papillae are home to dense concentrations of sensory neurons, which transcode physical contact and the presence of chemical molecules in the water into the sensory modalities of touch and olfaction. A slug can have around two to three dozen papillae, which can be easily seen with the naked eye. These papillae run along the entire length of the oral veil, all the way to the "tentacles", the long tapering tips that give the oral veil a somewhat English-moustache-like look.

Two bilateral pairs of sensory nerves innervate the oral veil: the Large Oral Veil Nerve (LOVN) and the Tentacle Nerve (TN). The LOVN and the TN collect sensory input from the chemotactile papillae on the edge of the oral veil. Each single nerve only innervates half of the oral veil, between medial sites and the lateral tentacle tip. Sensory information is sent to the central turn motor network for motor patterning.

Three critical points:

  1. LOVN spiking responses to sensory stimulation is greatest at the most medial sites and declines toward tentacle tip. Conversely, TN sensitivity is greatest at the tentacle and declines toward the median.
  2. If two or more sites on the oral veil are stimulated simultaneously, both the turning response and the spiking responses in LOVN and TN average the input. Thus, the responses encode a most likely direction of the source for prey tracking and biting attack.
  3. Sensory inputs to either side of the oral veil are averaged in the peripheral nervous system before being sent to the CNS. Bilateral sensory inputs are averaged together in the CNS, in the turn network; but, on either side of the slug, multiple stimuli are integrated directly in the neural structures of the oral veil itself.
